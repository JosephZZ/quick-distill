% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================================
\begin{abstract}
On-policy knowledge distillation applies reverse KL divergence loss uniformly across all generated tokens. We show this full-sequence approach is unstable: students learn degenerate repetition patterns, causing HumanEval accuracy to drop from 40.2\% to 26.8\% over training. Analyzing per-position KL divergence across 59,936 trajectories, we find it is highly non-uniform---KL concentrates at early positions corresponding to reasoning strategy selection, while later computation and formatting tokens show minimal divergence. We propose \method{}: compute loss only on the first $N$ response tokens. This one-line change matches or exceeds full-sequence performance on both mathematical reasoning (MATH-500: 66.75\% vs.\ 65.55\% avg@4) and code generation (HumanEval: 42.1\% vs.\ 40.2\% pass@1), while eliminating training instability entirely. A strong \emph{cascade effect} drives the improvement: distilling just 50 tokens reshapes entire 1500+ character responses, with late tokens changing as much as early ones (Jaccard similarity 0.251 for both early and late portions). The first 50 tokens capture only 26\% of total KL signal yet achieve 73\% of the maximum performance gain. Positional distillation is also 3--7$\times$ more efficient in generation, as only $N$ tokens need to be produced per training step.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Knowledge distillation~\citep{hinton2015distilling} has become a central technique for compressing large language models (LLMs)~\citep{xu2024survey}. On-policy distillation---where the student generates responses and the teacher provides distributional supervision---is particularly effective for autoregressive models~\citep{agarwal2024onpolicy, gu2024minillm}, as the student learns from its own mistakes rather than from teacher-generated data. However, the standard approach applies reverse KL divergence loss \emph{uniformly} across every token position, implicitly assuming all tokens contribute equally to learning.

This assumption is wrong. In our experiments with Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B and Qwen3-1.7B~\citep{qwen2024qwen25math, qwen2025qwen3}, full-sequence on-policy distillation causes 91.5\% of math responses to develop repetitive \bboxed{} patterns by step 100, and HumanEval accuracy drops from 40.2\% to 26.8\% over training. The root cause is uniform loss weighting: it wastes gradient capacity on low-information positions and creates degenerate attractors at high-confidence answer tokens.

We investigate by measuring per-position KL divergence across 59,936 on-policy trajectories. The result is striking: KL is \emph{highly front-loaded}. Position 0 has KL = 3.057---8.5$\times$ higher than position 192 (KL = 0.359). Token classification reveals why: early positions are dominated by \emph{reasoning strategy tokens} (``To find...'', ``First...'') where teacher and student fundamentally disagree, while later positions contain \emph{computation tokens} (numbers, operators) where the models largely agree~\citep{wang2025beyond8020}.

Based on this analysis, we propose \methodfull{}: compute the reverse KL loss only on the first $N$ response tokens---a single-line modification that zeros out gradients beyond position $N$. Despite its simplicity, the consequences are significant. Our contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{KL position analysis.} We show that KL divergence in on-policy distillation is highly position-dependent: teacher-student disagreement concentrates at early reasoning-strategy tokens, with the first 50 tokens containing 26\% of total KL (Section~\ref{sec:analysis}).
    \item \textbf{\methodfull{}.} Computing loss on the first $N$ tokens matches or exceeds full-sequence performance on MATH-500 (66.75\% vs.\ 65.55\% avg@4) and HumanEval (42.1\% vs.\ 40.2\% pass@1), while completely eliminating training instability and reducing generation cost 3--7$\times$ (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
    \item \textbf{Cascade effect.} Early-token distillation reshapes entire generation trajectories: distilling 50 tokens changes late tokens as much as early ones (Jaccard similarity 0.251 for both), with a 3:1 ratio of improved to degraded problems (Section~\ref{sec:cascade}).
    \item \textbf{Comprehensive evaluation.} Consistent benefits across tasks (math, coding), training methods (LoRA, full fine-tuning), batch configurations, and position limits from 5 to 200 (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Knowledge Distillation for LLMs}

Knowledge distillation~\citep{hinton2015distilling} trains a student to match a teacher's output distribution. \citet{kim2016sequence} extended this to sequence models, and \citet{wen2023fdivergence} generalized to arbitrary $f$-divergences. For LLMs, \citet{gu2024minillm} established that reverse KL is preferable for generative distillation, as it prevents the student from overestimating low-probability teacher modes, while \citet{wu2025rethinking} showed that both divergences converge given sufficient training but focus on different distribution regions early on.

The on-policy paradigm---where the student generates its own training data---outperforms off-policy approaches~\citep{agarwal2024onpolicy, lin2020autoregressive}. \citet{agarwal2024onpolicy} proposed Generalized Knowledge Distillation (GKD), and \citet{ko2024distillm} introduced skew KL for more efficient distillation. Our work builds on on-policy reverse KL but introduces position-dependent loss computation.

\subsection{Token-Level Importance in Distillation}
\label{sec:related_token}

Not all tokens contribute equally to learning. \citet{wang2025beyond8020} showed that only $\sim$20\% of chain-of-thought tokens carry high entropy, and training RL gradients on only these ``forking tokens'' matches full-gradient performance. This motivated several concurrent approaches to selective distillation: \citet{huang2025selectkd} proposed SelecTKD (propose-and-verify token selection), \citet{xie2026adakd} introduced AdaKD (adaptive per-token temperature), \citet{kim2026tsdkd} combined entropy-based selection with preference ranking, and \citet{tavor2026rethinking} systematically disentangled selection along position, class, and sample axes, proposing SE-KD which reduces wall time by 70\%.

Our approach differs fundamentally: we use \emph{absolute position} as the sole selection criterion, requiring no per-token entropy, difficulty, or verification computation. The KL concentration we observe at early positions provides a principled justification---early tokens correspond to reasoning strategy decisions where teacher-student disagreement is highest. Furthermore, unlike prior token-selection methods that primarily target efficiency, we show that positional loss also eliminates training instability.

\citet{chen2025distillingessence} studied computation-quality tradeoffs in reasoning distillation via sequence truncation. While they truncate training sequences from the end, we restrict the \emph{loss} to the beginning while retaining full generation for inference.

\subsection{Training Instability in On-Policy Distillation}
\label{sec:related_stability}

Neural text degeneration~\citep{holtzman2020curious, welleck2020neural} and model collapse from iterative self-training~\citep{shumailov2024collapse} are well-documented failure modes. In on-policy KD specifically, \citet{jang2026veto} proposed Veto, a geometric bridge in logit space that suppresses harmful gradients on low-confidence tokens. Our approach is complementary: rather than modifying the target distribution, we restrict loss to early positions where teacher-student agreement is naturally highest, achieving stability through a simpler mechanism.

\subsection{Curriculum Learning and Sequence Length Scheduling}
\label{sec:related_curriculum}

Curriculum learning~\citep{bengio2009curriculum} improves training by ordering examples from easy to hard. \citet{li2022stability} showed that sequence length warmup stabilizes LLM training, and \citet{pouransari2024dataset} extended this with variable-length curricula for up to 3$\times$ faster convergence. Our progressive variant---linearly increasing the position limit over training---applies curriculum learning to the distillation loss scope rather than input length.

\subsection{Chain-of-Thought and Reasoning Distillation}

Chain-of-thought prompting~\citep{wei2022chainofthought} enables LLMs to solve complex problems via intermediate reasoning. Distilling CoT capabilities has become a key application of KD~\citep{deepseekr1}. Process reward models~\citep{lightman2023verify} provide step-level supervision. Our work connects to this line: \method{} implicitly provides strongest supervision on \emph{planning} steps at early positions, with diminishing supervision on later computation---consistent with the intuition that reasoning \emph{strategy} is harder to learn than arithmetic.

\section{Motivation: Full-Sequence Distillation Fails}
\label{sec:motivation}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We use Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B~\citep{qwen2024qwen25math} as the student and Qwen3-1.7B~\citep{qwen2025qwen3} as the teacher. The distillation pipeline follows the on-policy paradigm: (1) the student generates responses to math/coding problems, (2) the teacher computes token-level log-probabilities via a forward pass, (3) the reverse KL divergence loss is computed and used to update the student via LoRA~\citep{hu2022lora} ($r=32$, $\alpha=64$, lr=$5\times10^{-5}$) or full fine-tuning (lr=$5\times10^{-6}$). We evaluate on MATH-500~\citep{hendrycks2021measuring, lightman2023verify} (avg@4, maj@4, pass@4 with $n=4$ samples at temperature 0.7) and HumanEval/MBPP~\citep{chen2021evaluating, austin2021program} (pass@1 at temperature 0.0).

\subsection{The Degradation Problem}

Full-sequence distillation exhibits severe degradation over training on both tasks.

\paragraph{Mathematical reasoning.} By step 100, 91.5\% of responses develop multi-\bboxed{} repetition, averaging 88 repeated answer boxes per response. The apparent avg@4 drops from 65.6\% (step 50) to 43.8\% (step 200). A relaxed metric counting any response containing the ground truth reveals that mathematical ability is largely preserved: relaxed avg@4 drops only from 74.6\% to 70.3\%. Thus $\sim$80\% of the apparent degradation is caused by degenerate repetition breaking answer extraction, not genuine loss of reasoning ability.

\paragraph{Code generation.} The degradation is more severe and genuine. With LoRA, HumanEval drops monotonically from 40.2\% (step 50) to 26.8\% (step 400)---a 13.4 percentage point decline. With full fine-tuning, HumanEval drops from 36.6\% (step 150) to 31.1\% (step 400). Unlike math, code generation degradation cannot be explained by extraction artifacts.

\paragraph{Positional variants are immune.} None of the positional distillation variants (pos-50 through pos-200) exhibit degradation on either task. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:degradation}, positional loss maintains stable \bboxed{} counts (1.0 per response), low repetition rates (5.8\%), and consistent accuracy throughout training. Figure~\ref{fig:stability} illustrates this contrast across training steps.

\section{Analysis: Where Does KL Divergence Concentrate?}
\label{sec:analysis}

The failure of full-sequence distillation and the success of positional variants suggest that not all token positions are equally important for distillation. We investigate this by analyzing per-position KL divergence between teacher and student.

\subsection{Per-Position KL Distribution}

We generate 59,936 on-policy trajectories (mean sequence length 386 tokens) from the undistilled student and compute per-position KL divergence against the teacher. The results (Figure~\ref{fig:kl_curve}) reveal a striking pattern: KL is massively concentrated at early positions.

    \label{fig:kl_curve}
\end{figure}

The decay is rapid in the first 5 positions (3.057 $\rightarrow$ 0.921), then gradual, stabilizing after position $\sim$50.

To understand how KL signal accumulates, we compute the fraction of per-trajectory total KL contained in the first $N$ positions. This sub-linearity is the core insight motivating \method{}: early KL is disproportionately valuable for downstream task performance.

\subsection{Token Classification Analysis}
\label{sec:token_classification}

To understand \emph{why} KL concentrates at early positions, we classify each token into six categories: \texttt{planning} (reasoning keywords such as ``To'', ``First'', ``Therefore''), \texttt{structural} (punctuation, newlines, formatting markers), \texttt{math\_number} (digits), \texttt{math\_operator} (arithmetic operators), \texttt{math\_latex} (LaTeX delimiters and math keywords), and \texttt{continuation} (all other tokens).

Table~\ref{tab:kl_by_category} shows mean KL by category and position range. Three findings emerge:

\paragraph{Finding 1: Early positions are strategic, late positions are mechanical.} At positions 0--4, 33\% of tokens are planning tokens (strategy selection), with only 2\% math numbers. By positions 200--500, planning tokens drop to 8\% while math numbers rise to 18\% and structural tokens to 39\%. Early positions contain \emph{what approach to use}; later positions contain \emph{executing the computation}.

\paragraph{Finding 2: KL concentrates on decision-point tokens.} Planning tokens have a mean KL of 1.754 across all positions, compared to 0.281 for math numbers---a 6.2$\times$ difference. The teacher and student disagree most on \emph{how to structure reasoning}, not on \emph{what to compute}. At position 0, 94.4\% of tokens are planning tokens (``To'' appearing 75.1\% of the time, ``Let'' 10.1\%, ``First'' 3.4\%).

\paragraph{Finding 3: LaTeX delimiters carry the highest per-token KL.} The tokens \texttt{\textbackslash(} and \texttt{\textbackslash[} have mean KL of 10.3 and 13.2 respectively, reflecting fundamental disagreement about math formatting conventions. This suggests that a substantial fraction of KL divergence comes from formatting preferences rather than reasoning content.

\subsection{Post-Distillation KL Verification}
\label{sec:post_distill_kl}

If positional distillation only improved the trained positions, we would expect KL reduction limited to positions 0--$N$. Instead, we find a cascade effect at the distributional level. Table~\ref{tab:post_kl} compares per-position KL between the raw student and two distilled variants (pos-200tok and full-seq) against the teacher.

Within the trained range (positions 0--200), pos-200tok reduces KL by 50--60\% relative to the raw student. Crucially, in the untrained range (positions 200--400), KL still drops by 30--40\%. This confirms that early-token distillation alters the student's behavior at later positions, consistent with the autoregressive cascade mechanism: better early tokens condition the model into distributions closer to the teacher throughout the sequence.

\section{Method: Positional Distillation}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Formulation}

In standard on-policy reverse KL distillation, the student $p_\theta$ generates a response $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_T)$ conditioned on a prompt, and the loss is computed against the teacher $q_\phi$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{full}} = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p_\theta} \left[ \sum_{t=1}^{T} \KL\!\left(q_\phi(\cdot \mid \mathbf{x}_{<t}) \;\|\; p_\theta(\cdot \mid \mathbf{x}_{<t})\right) \right].
\label{eq:full_loss}
\end{equation}

\Method{} modifies this by restricting the loss to the first $N$ response tokens:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{pos}} = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p_\theta} \left[ \sum_{t=1}^{N} \KL\!\left(q_\phi(\cdot \mid \mathbf{x}_{<t}) \;\|\; p_\theta(\cdot \mid \mathbf{x}_{<t})\right) \right],
\label{eq:pos_loss}
\end{equation}
where $N \ll T$ is the position limit. In practice, the student only needs to generate $N$ tokens (rather than the full sequence $T$), and the teacher only needs to score these $N$ tokens. The implementation is a single mask applied to the loss tensor---positions beyond $N$ are zeroed out before the backward pass.

\subsection{Efficiency Benefits}

\Method{} provides efficiency gains at three stages of the training pipeline:

\paragraph{Generation.} The student generates only $N$ tokens per training example instead of a full sequence. For $N=50$ versus a typical full-sequence length of $T=3584$, this is a 71$\times$ reduction in generated tokens. Since autoregressive generation is the bottleneck of on-policy distillation (each token requires a full forward pass), this translates directly to proportional wall-clock savings.

\paragraph{Teacher scoring.} The teacher's forward pass processes only $N$ tokens instead of $T$, reducing compute quadratically (for attention) or linearly (for feed-forward layers) relative to sequence length.

\paragraph{Loss and gradient computation.} The loss is summed over $N$ positions instead of $T$, and gradients flow through $N$ token predictions instead of $T$.

\subsection{Variants}

We explore three variants:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fixed pos-$N$}: Compute loss on the first $N$ tokens throughout training. The simplest approach and the primary recommendation.
    \item \textbf{Progressive}: Linearly increase the position limit from 1 to a maximum value over the course of training. This creates a natural curriculum but forfeits the generation efficiency benefit as $N$ grows.
    \item \textbf{Position-weighted} (future work): Weight the loss at each position proportional to expected KL, rather than using a hard cutoff.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:pos_distill} summarizes the training procedure. The only modifications to standard on-policy distillation are the generation length (line~\ref{line:gen}) and the loss summation range (line~\ref{line:loss}).

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Positional Distillation}
\label{alg:pos_distill}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Student $p_\theta$, teacher $q_\phi$, position limit $N$, problem set $\mathcal{D}$
\FOR{each training step}
    \STATE Sample batch of problems $\{c_i\}$ from $\mathcal{D}$
    \STATE Generate $\mathbf{x}_i \sim p_\theta(\cdot \mid c_i)$, $|\mathbf{x}_i| = N$ \label{line:gen} \COMMENT{Generate only $N$ tokens}
    \STATE Compute teacher log-probs: $\log q_\phi(x_{i,t} \mid \mathbf{x}_{i,<t})$ for $t \in [1, N]$
    \STATE Compute student log-probs: $\log p_\theta(x_{i,t} \mid \mathbf{x}_{i,<t})$ for $t \in [1, N]$
    \STATE $\mathcal{L} \leftarrow \frac{1}{|\text{batch}|} \sum_i \sum_{t=1}^{N} \KL(q_\phi \| p_\theta)(\mathbf{x}_{i,<t})$ \label{line:loss} \COMMENT{Loss on first $N$ only}
    \STATE Update $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

\paragraph{Models.} Student: Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B~\citep{qwen2024qwen25math}; Teacher: Qwen3-1.7B~\citep{qwen2025qwen3}.

\paragraph{Training.} Two adaptation methods: (1) LoRA~\citep{hu2022lora} with $r=32$, $\alpha=64$, targeting all linear layers (q/k/v/o/gate/up/down projections), lr = $5\times10^{-5}$; (2) full fine-tuning (FullFT) with lr = $5\times10^{-6}$. Temperature 0.7 for on-policy generation. Cosine learning rate schedule.

\paragraph{Math experiments.} 3,200 training problems, $n_{\text{samples}}=1$, batch size 16, 200 training steps. Position limits: 50, 100, 200, and full-sequence. Evaluation: MATH-500~\citep{hendrycks2021measuring} with $n=4$ samples at temperature 0.7, reporting avg@4, maj@4, and pass@4.

\paragraph{Coding experiments.} 6,400 training problems from CodeUltraFeedback, $n_{\text{samples}}=1$, batch size 16, 400 training steps. Position limits: 50, 100, 150, 250, and full-sequence. Evaluation: HumanEval, HumanEval+, MBPP, MBPP+~\citep{chen2021evaluating, austin2021program} at temperature 0.0.

\subsection{The Cascade Effect}
\label{sec:cascade}

A natural concern is that restricting loss to the first $N$ tokens might only improve those specific positions without benefiting the overall response. We provide five lines of evidence that this is not the case---early-token distillation creates a strong \emph{cascade effect} that improves the entire generation trajectory.

\paragraph{Evidence 1: Downstream accuracy far exceeds trained positions.} Pos-5, which distills only the first 5 tokens, improves avg@4 from 50.9\% to 56.3\% (+5.4pp). Pos-50 improves to 61.9\% (+11.0pp). These accuracy gains manifest across the full response (averaging 1,929 characters), not just the first 50 tokens. The improvement ratio is 3:1---202 out of 500 problems improve, while only 68 degrade.

\paragraph{Evidence 2: Late tokens change as much as early tokens.} We compare distilled and base model responses using Jaccard word similarity, split at word position 40 ($\approx$50 tokens). For pos-50, the Jaccard similarity to baseline is 0.251 for both the early and late portions. Late tokens---which receive zero distillation signal---change just as much as early tokens. At a word cutoff of 150 ($\approx$195 tokens), the late Jaccard is actually lower (0.202 vs.\ 0.265), meaning late tokens change \emph{more} than early tokens.

\paragraph{Evidence 3: KL reduction beyond trained positions.} As shown in Table~\ref{tab:post_kl}, pos-200tok reduces KL by 50--60\% in the trained range (0--200) and by 30--40\% in the untrained range (200--400), confirming the cascade at the distribution level.

\paragraph{Evidence 4: Structural and behavioral changes.} Distilled models adopt teacher-like response structure throughout the entire response. Pos-50 produces responses with 5.3 step markers per response (vs.\ 2.8 for base), and all positional models converge to ``We are given...'' openings (10.5\% of responses for pos-50, vs.\ diverse openings for the base model). These structural changes extend far beyond the first 50 tokens.

\paragraph{Evidence 5: Training stability.} Full-sequence distillation, which trains on all positions, paradoxically produces less stable training than positional methods that train on fewer positions. This suggests that the cascade effect from early-token training is more beneficial than direct supervision of later positions, which introduces degenerate attractor states.

\subsection{LoRA vs.\ Full Fine-Tuning}

Across all experiments, LoRA consistently outperforms full fine-tuning on mathematical reasoning by approximately 10 percentage points (e.g., pos-50: 66.65\% vs.\ 56.75\%). We hypothesize that LoRA's low-rank constraint provides beneficial regularization that prevents overfitting to the distillation loss.

An important nuance: with FullFT, full-sequence distillation (58.20\%) outperforms positional variants (best 56.75\%), and does not exhibit catastrophic repetition. This suggests that \method{}'s primary benefit is \emph{stability under aggressive optimization}. With LoRA's higher learning rate ($5\times10^{-5}$), full-sequence loss creates degenerate attractors that positional loss avoids. With FullFT's conservative learning rate ($5\times10^{-6}$), these attractors do not form, and full-sequence loss provides marginally more signal. Since LoRA is the dominant adaptation method for LLM distillation in practice, this is the more relevant regime.

On coding tasks, the picture is mixed: LoRA achieves higher HumanEval scores (42.1\% vs.\ 37.8\%), while FullFT achieves higher MBPP scores (54.8\% vs.\ 52.1\%). Both training methods benefit from \method{}'s stability advantage over full-sequence distillation.

\subsection{Batch Configuration}
\label{sec:batch_config}

We compare three batch configurations for LoRA math experiments: $n16\text{bs}16$ (1 problem $\times$ 16 trajectories per step), $n1\text{bs}16$ (16 problems $\times$ 1 trajectory per step), and $n1\text{bs}1$ (1 problem $\times$ 1 trajectory per step).

\paragraph{LoRA.} The two batch-size-16 configurations achieve comparable peak performance ($n16$: 66.75\%, $n1$: 66.65\%), suggesting that problem diversity and trajectory diversity contribute roughly equally when combined with LoRA's implicit regularization.

\paragraph{Full fine-tuning.} $n1\text{bs}16$ consistently outperforms $n16\text{bs}16$ by $\sim$2 percentage points (56.75\% vs.\ 54.30\% for pos-50), indicating that full fine-tuning benefits more from problem diversity than trajectory diversity.

\paragraph{Extreme: $n1\text{bs}1$.} Despite single-trajectory gradients (extremely noisy), training with batch size 1 does not diverge. With 16$\times$ more steps (3,200 total), pos-50 reaches 80.4\% pass@4, surpassing the batch-size-16 variant's 78.6\%. LoRA's low-rank constraint provides sufficient implicit regularization even for this extreme regime.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Why Does Positional Distillation Work?}

We propose three complementary explanations for the effectiveness of \method{}:

\paragraph{Autoregressive cascade.} Early tokens determine the trajectory of subsequent tokens. Better initial decisions---approach selection, problem framing---propagate through generation. Our Jaccard analysis (Section~\ref{sec:cascade}) confirms this: changing the first 50 tokens changes the entire 1500+ character response uniformly.

\paragraph{Strategy over arithmetic.} Token classification (Section~\ref{sec:token_classification}) shows teacher-student disagreement concentrates on planning tokens (mean KL = 1.754) vs.\ computation tokens (0.281)---a 6.2$\times$ ratio. The teacher's value is in knowing \emph{how to approach} problems, not \emph{how to compute} answers. Distilling strategy is sufficient; distilling computation adds noise.

\paragraph{Avoiding degenerate attractors.} After producing $\texttt{\textbackslash boxed\{answer\}}$, full-sequence loss encourages repeating this high-confidence token, creating a positive feedback loop. \Method{} avoids this entirely by not computing loss beyond early positions.

\subsection{Limitations}

Our work has several limitations that warrant future investigation:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Single model pair.} All experiments use Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B / Qwen3-1.7B. While consistent across two tasks and two training methods, generalization to other families and scales remains to be validated.
    \item \textbf{Position limit selection.} The optimal $N$ varies by task (50--200 works broadly). Adaptive methods setting $N$ per-example could be more robust.
    \item \textbf{Task generality.} The cascade effect relies on early tokens being ``strategic.'' For tasks without this structure (e.g., translation), the method may be less effective.
    \item \textbf{Single random seed.} Consistency across configurations provides confidence, but formal significance testing with multiple seeds would strengthen claims.
    \item \textbf{Optimizer interaction.} With conservative optimization (FullFT, lr=$5\times10^{-6}$), full-sequence distillation outperforms positional variants. \Method{} is most beneficial under aggressive optimization (LoRA, lr=$5\times10^{-5}$)---the dominant regime in practice but not universal.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Broader Impact}

\Method{} reduces generation cost per training step by 3--7$\times$, making on-policy distillation practical where full-sequence generation is prohibitive. It also improves stability, reducing the need for careful early stopping. Beyond distillation, the finding that KL divergence is front-loaded may inform reward design in RLHF~\citep{deepseekr1} and RL for reasoning~\citep{wang2025beyond8020}, where similar positional effects likely exist.


% ============================================================================
% 8. CONCLUSION
% ============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}

We showed that on-policy knowledge distillation suffers from positional non-uniformity: teacher-student KL divergence concentrates at early reasoning-strategy tokens, with the first 50 tokens containing 26\% of total KL yet delivering 73\% of the performance gain. \methodfull{} exploits this by computing loss only on the first $N$ response tokens---a single-line change that matches or exceeds full-sequence performance on MATH-500 (66.75\% vs.\ 65.55\% avg@4) and HumanEval (42.1\% vs.\ 40.2\% pass@1), eliminates training instability, and reduces generation cost by 3--7$\times$. The cascade effect---where distilling 50 early tokens reshapes entire 1500+ token responses---demonstrates that in autoregressive models, getting the beginning right is sufficient to get everything right.

\section{Token Classification Methodology}
\label{app:token_classification}

We classify each token in the student's vocabulary into six categories based on string matching rules applied to the decoded token text:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{planning}: Reasoning/strategy keywords that indicate approach selection. Includes tokens matching ``To'', ``Let'', ``First'', ``Step'', ``We'', ``Given'', ``Therefore'', ``Thus'', ``Since'', ``Solution'', ``Next'', ``From'', and their lowercase variants. These tokens reflect \emph{what reasoning approach to use}.

    \item \textbf{structural}: Punctuation, whitespace, and formatting tokens. Includes commas, colons, newlines, bold markers (\texttt{**}), dollar signs, parentheses, and similar. These tokens organize the response structure.

    \item \textbf{math\_number}: Digit tokens (0--9). These carry mathematical content but show minimal teacher-student disagreement.

    \item \textbf{math\_operator}: Arithmetic and relational operators ($+$, $-$, $\times$, $/$, $=$, $<$, $>$, $\hat{~}$). Like numbers, these show low KL.

    \item \textbf{math\_latex}: LaTeX delimiters and mathematical keywords (\texttt{\textbackslash(}, \texttt{\textbackslash[}, \texttt{\textbackslash)}, ``sum'', ``fraction'', ``integer'', etc.). These show surprisingly high KL, reflecting formatting convention differences between models.

    \item \textbf{continuation}: All tokens not matching the above categories. This is the largest category, containing general English words, word fragments, and domain-specific terms.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Degradation Pattern}

The typical degradation pattern is: the model produces a correct solution and answer, then fills the remaining token budget with repetitions of the conclusion (e.g., ``This is the final answer. $\backslash$boxed\{answer\}'' repeated 30--80 times). This is consistent with a degenerate attractor in the full-sequence loss landscape: after producing a high-confidence answer token, the gradient from the teacher's distribution encourages producing the same high-confidence token again, creating a positive feedback loop that \method{} avoids entirely by not computing loss on answer-presentation positions.
