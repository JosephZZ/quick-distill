% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================================
\begin{abstract}
On-policy knowledge distillation trains the student on its own rollouts while the teacher provides token-level soft targets. In practice, we observe two failures: (1) performance degrades after extended training, with students developing degenerate repetition patterns, and (2) full-length rollout generation at each step is the dominant computational cost. To investigate, we analyze per-position KL divergence over on-policy trajectories. The distillation signal is heavily front-loaded: the first 100 tokens account for 44\% of total KL, while remaining positions form a long, low-signal tail. We hypothesize that continued optimization on these low-signal positions contributes to instability, and that the first $N$ tokens carry sufficient signal for effective distillation. We propose Positional-OPD: rollout just first $N$ response tokens distill on these only. Experiments on mathematical reasoning (MATH-500), code generation (HumanEval/MBPP), and function calling (BFCL), across multiple teacher scales (1.7B, 4B, 8B) and model families (Qwen, Gemma), show that Positional-OPD matches or exceeds full-sequence distillation while eliminating training instability and reducing generation cost.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

On-policy knowledge distillation (KD) trains the student on its own rollouts while the teacher supplies token-level soft targets~\citep{agarwal2024onpolicy, gu2024minillm}. Recent work has shown that this paradigm can substantially outperform off-policy KD on large language models~\citep{agarwal2024onpolicy, xu2025speculative}, because loss is computed on the student's own distribution rather than on teacher-generated sequences, reducing the train-inference distribution shift~\citep{lin2020autoregressive, deepseekr1}.

In our experiments with on-policy reverse-KL KD, we observe two practical failures. \textbf{First, performance degrades after extended training.} Rather than monotonically improving, performance peaks early and then declines: on HumanEval, pass@1 drops from 40.24\% (step 50) to 26.83\% (step 400); on MATH-500, 91.5\% of responses contain degenerate repetition by step 100. This is consistent with continued on-policy optimization pushing the student into a degenerate attractor. \textbf{Second, rollout generation is the computational bottleneck.} Each on-policy step requires generating full rollouts before computing gradients~\citep{agarwal2024onpolicy, gu2024minillm}, making autoregressive generation the dominant cost and limiting the scalability of on-policy KD.

These failures motivate a concrete question: \emph{do later tokens contribute meaningful KD signal, or are early tokens sufficient?} We therefore analyze the per-position KL divergence between teacher and student distributions along student rollouts. KL provides a first-order proxy for the magnitude of token-level disagreement. Analyzing 59,936 on-policy trajectories, we find that the signal is strikingly front-loaded: position 0 has KL = 3.06, which is 8.5$\times$ higher than position 192 (KL = 0.36). The first 100 tokens contribute 43.9\% of total KL; beyond $\sim$100 tokens, KL flattens into a long tail.

This pattern suggests a hypothesis. We hypothesize that continued optimization on low-KL tail positions contributes to instability via noisy or misaligned gradients---where teacher and student already largely agree, the gradient signal carries little meaningful teaching, and prolonged fitting to these positions may distort the student's parameters. Meanwhile, the first $N$ tokens, which carry the majority of the KL signal, may be sufficient for effective knowledge transfer.

We test a minimalist intervention: \textbf{restrict KD loss to the first $N$ generated tokens}. This requires only masking the loss at position $N$; optionally, rollouts can also be truncated to $N$ tokens for efficiency. We call this approach \methodfull{}.

Experiments provide support across three tasks---mathematical reasoning (MATH-500), code generation (HumanEval/MBPP), and function calling (BFCL)---and across multiple teacher scales (Qwen3-1.7B, 4B, 8B) and model families (Qwen, Llama). With $N{=}100$, \method{} consistently matches or exceeds the best performance of full-sequence distillation while preventing the training degradation observed with full-sequence loss. Truncating rollouts to $N$ tokens additionally reduces generation cost (measured wall-clock speedups reported in Section~\ref{sec:efficiency}).

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Empirical analysis of the distillation signal.} We show that per-position KL divergence in on-policy KD is heavily front-loaded, with early positions dominated by reasoning-strategy tokens (high KL) and later positions by computation tokens (low KL) (Section~\ref{sec:analysis}).
    \item \textbf{\methodfull{}.} A method that restricts KD loss to the first $N$ response tokens, eliminating training instability and enabling truncated rollouts for efficiency (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
    \item \textbf{Evaluation across tasks, scales, and model families.} Consistent improvements on math, coding, and function calling, with multiple Qwen teacher sizes and cross-family validation (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Preliminary Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{The Degradation Problem}
\label{sec:degradation}

We first establish that full-sequence on-policy distillation suffers from severe training instability. Using Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B as the student and Qwen3-1.7B as the teacher, we train with reverse KL divergence loss applied uniformly across all generated token positions.

\paragraph{Mathematical reasoning.} On MATH-500, full-sequence distillation with LoRA peaks at 65.55\% avg@4 at step 50, then degrades. By step 100, 91.5\% of responses develop multi-\bboxed{} repetition, averaging 88 repeated answer boxes per response. The apparent avg@4 drops to 46.30\%. A relaxed metric counting any response containing the ground truth reveals that $\sim$80\% of the apparent degradation is caused by degenerate repetition breaking answer extraction, not genuine loss of reasoning ability---but even by this relaxed measure, performance still degrades from 74.60\% to 70.30\%.

\paragraph{Code generation.} The degradation is more severe and genuine. With LoRA, HumanEval drops monotonically from 40.24\% (step 50) to 26.83\% (step 400)---a 33\% relative decline that cannot be explained by extraction artifacts.

\paragraph{Function calling.} Full-sequence distillation on BFCL peaks at 58.20\% full accuracy at step 100, then oscillates and declines. The instability, while less catastrophic than math or coding, still limits practical deployment.

These degradation patterns motivate the central question of this paper: \emph{what in the distillation signal causes this instability?}

\subsection{Token-Type Decomposition}
\label{sec:token_classification}

To understand \emph{why} KL concentrates at early positions, we classify each token into six categories: \texttt{planning} (reasoning keywords: ``To'', ``First'', ``Therefore''), \texttt{structural} (punctuation, formatting), \texttt{math\_number} (digits), \texttt{math\_operator} (arithmetic operators), \texttt{math\_latex} (LaTeX delimiters), and \texttt{continuation} (all others).

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{figures/fig_token_composition.pdf}
    \caption{\textbf{Token-type composition by position.} Early positions are dominated by planning/strategy tokens, while later positions shift to structural formatting and computation.}
    \label{fig:token_composition}
\end{figure}

Three findings emerge:

\paragraph{Early positions are strategic, late positions are mechanical.} At positions 0--4, 33\% of tokens are planning tokens (strategy selection), with only 2\% math numbers. By positions 200--500, planning drops to 8\% while math numbers rise to 18\% and structural tokens to 39\%. Early positions encode \emph{what approach to use}; later positions encode \emph{executing the computation}.

\paragraph{KL concentrates on decision-point tokens.} Planning tokens have mean KL of 1.754 across all positions, compared to 0.281 for math numbers---a 6.2$\times$ difference. At position 0, 94.4\% of tokens are planning tokens (``To'' appearing 75.1\%, ``Let'' 10.1\%, ``First'' 3.4\%). The teacher and student disagree most on \emph{how to structure reasoning}, not on \emph{what to compute}.

\paragraph{LaTeX delimiters carry high KL.} The tokens \texttt{\textbackslash(} and \texttt{\textbackslash[} have mean KL of 10.3 and 13.2, reflecting formatting convention differences between models. This suggests that a portion of the KL divergence at certain positions reflects formatting preferences rather than reasoning content.


\subsection{Hypothesis}
\label{sec:hypothesis}

The analysis above reveals that the distillation signal is concentrated in the first $\sim$100 tokens, corresponding to reasoning strategy and approach selection. The long tail of low-KL positions contributes minimal learning signal. We hypothesize that:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Overfitting to low-signal positions causes degradation.} When KL is already small, the gradient signal is dominated by noise rather than meaningful teaching. Over many training steps, these noisy gradients accumulate and distort the student's parameters, leading to the degenerate repetition patterns observed in Section~\ref{sec:degradation}.
    \item \textbf{The first $N$ tokens carry sufficient signal.} Since early tokens encode reasoning strategy---the aspect where teacher and student disagree most---distilling only these tokens should be sufficient for effective knowledge transfer.
\end{enumerate}

If both hypotheses hold, then restricting distillation to the first $N$ tokens should simultaneously solve the degradation problem and reduce rollout cost, since only $N$ tokens need to be generated per training step.

\section{Method: Positional Distillation}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Formulation}

In standard on-policy reverse KL distillation, the student $p_\theta$ generates a response $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_T)$ conditioned on a prompt, and the loss is computed against the teacher $q_\phi$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{full}} = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p_\theta} \left[ \sum_{t=1}^{T} \KL\!\left(q_\phi(\cdot \mid \mathbf{x}_{<t}) \;\|\; p_\theta(\cdot \mid \mathbf{x}_{<t})\right) \right].
\label{eq:full_loss}
\end{equation}

\Method{} restricts the loss to the first $N$ response tokens:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{pos}} = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p_\theta} \left[ \sum_{t=1}^{N} \KL\!\left(q_\phi(\cdot \mid \mathbf{x}_{<t}) \;\|\; p_\theta(\cdot \mid \mathbf{x}_{<t})\right) \right],
\label{eq:pos_loss}
\end{equation}
where $N$ is a fixed position cutoff ($N \ll T$ in practice). If the student generates an EOS token before position $N$, loss is computed only up to EOS.

Note that restricting the loss to the first $N$ tokens is conceptually distinct from truncating the rollout to $N$ tokens. In our implementation, we do both: we generate only $N$ tokens \emph{and} compute loss only on these tokens. This couples two interventions---loss masking and shorter generation. We observe that loss masking likely drives the stability benefit (by avoiding low-signal tail positions), while rollout truncation provides the efficiency benefit.

\subsection{Efficiency}
\label{sec:method_efficiency}

When rollouts are truncated to $N$ tokens, \method{} reduces cost at every stage:

\paragraph{Generation (dominant cost).} The student generates only $N$ tokens per example instead of $T$. For $N{=}100$ versus a maximum generation length of $T{=}3584$ (mean rollout length 386 tokens), this substantially reduces the number of generated tokens. Since autoregressive generation is the bottleneck of on-policy KD (each token requires a full forward pass), this translates to substantial wall-clock savings (measured in Section~\ref{sec:efficiency}).

\paragraph{Teacher scoring.} The teacher processes only $N$ tokens instead of $T$, reducing compute.

\paragraph{Loss computation.} Gradients flow through $N$ predictions instead of $T$.

\subsection{Algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:pos_distill} summarizes the procedure. The only modifications to standard on-policy distillation are the generation length (line~\ref{line:gen}) and the loss summation range (line~\ref{line:loss}).

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Positional Distillation}
\label{alg:pos_distill}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Student $p_\theta$, teacher $q_\phi$, position limit $N$, problem set $\mathcal{D}$
\FOR{each training step}
    \STATE Sample batch of problems $\{c_i\}$ from $\mathcal{D}$
    \STATE Generate $\mathbf{x}_i \sim p_\theta(\cdot \mid c_i)$, $|\mathbf{x}_i| = N$ \label{line:gen} \COMMENT{Generate only $N$ tokens}
    \STATE Compute teacher log-probs: $\log q_\phi(x_{i,t} \mid \mathbf{x}_{i,<t})$ for $t \in [1, N]$
    \STATE Compute student log-probs: $\log p_\theta(x_{i,t} \mid \mathbf{x}_{i,<t})$ for $t \in [1, N]$
    \STATE $\mathcal{L} \leftarrow \frac{1}{|\text{batch}|} \sum_i \sum_{t=1}^{N} \KL(q_\phi \| p_\theta)(\mathbf{x}_{i,<t})$ \label{line:loss} \COMMENT{Loss on first $N$ only}
    \STATE Update $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

\paragraph{Models.} We evaluate across multiple student-teacher pairs. Our primary student is Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B~\citep{qwen2024qwen25math}, paired with teachers of increasing scale: Qwen3-1.7B, Qwen3-4B, and Qwen3-8B~\citep{qwen2025qwen3}. To test cross-family generality, we also evaluate with \tbd{Llama student-teacher pair}.

\paragraph{Training.} All experiments use LoRA~\citep{hu2022lora} ($r{=}32$, $\alpha{=}64$, all linear layers, lr = $5{\times}10^{-5}$) with reverse KL divergence loss. Temperature 0.7 for on-policy generation. Each training step processes a batch of 16 problems with 1 rollout per problem ($n_{\text{samples}}{=}1$, batch size 16). We train for 200 steps on math and function calling, 400 steps on coding, saving checkpoints every 50 steps. Training data: 3,200 math problems, 6,400 coding problems (CodeUltraFeedback), and 3,200 function calling examples (glaive-function-calling-v2). Our method uses $N{=}100$ unless otherwise specified.

\paragraph{Evaluation.} MATH-500~\citep{hendrycks2021measuring, lightman2023verify} with $n{=}4$ samples at temperature 0.7 (reporting avg@4). HumanEval/HumanEval+ and MBPP/MBPP+~\citep{chen2021evaluating, austin2021program, liu2024evalplus} at temperature 0.0 (reporting pass@1). \subsection{LoRA vs.\ Full Fine-Tuning}
\label{sec:lora_vs_fullft}

We compare LoRA ($r{=}32$, lr = $5{\times}10^{-5}$) and full fine-tuning (lr = $5{\times}10^{-6}$) on both math and function calling.

\paragraph{Math.} LoRA consistently outperforms FullFT by $\sim$10 percentage points (e.g., pos-50: 66.65\% vs.\ 56.75\%). We attribute this to LoRA's low-rank constraint providing beneficial regularization. An important nuance: with FullFT, full-sequence distillation (58.20\%) slightly outperforms positional variants (best: 56.75\%) and does not exhibit catastrophic repetition. This suggests that \method{}'s primary benefit is stability under aggressive optimization---precisely the regime where LoRA (the dominant adaptation method) operates.

\paragraph{Function calling.} LoRA outperforms FullFT by 9.5--18.0pp across all position limits (e.g., pos-100: 61.30\% vs.\ 47.80\%). The larger gap compared to math suggests that LoRA's regularization is particularly important for format-sensitive tasks.

\paragraph{Both benefit from positional distillation.} Regardless of training paradigm, the positional insight holds: early-token loss is sufficient. The interaction between LoRA's implicit regularization and positional distillation's explicit scope restriction creates the strongest results.


\subsection{Batch Configuration}
\label{sec:batch_config}

We compare three batch configurations for LoRA math experiments: $n{=}16$, $\text{bs}{=}16$ (1 problem $\times$ 16 trajectories); $n{=}1$, $\text{bs}{=}16$ (16 problems $\times$ 1 trajectory); and $n{=}1$, $\text{bs}{=}1$ (1 problem $\times$ 1 trajectory).

The two batch-size-16 configurations achieve comparable peak performance ($n{=}16$: 66.75\%, $n{=}1$: 66.65\%), suggesting that problem diversity and trajectory diversity contribute roughly equally. Even the extreme $n{=}1$, $\text{bs}{=}1$ configuration (extremely noisy single-trajectory gradients) does not diverge under LoRA, reaching 80.4\% pass@4 with 3,200 steps. This demonstrates that \method{} is robust across batch configurations.


\subsection{Cascade Effect}
\label{sec:cascade}

A natural concern is that distilling only the first $N$ tokens might only improve those specific positions without benefiting the overall response. We find strong evidence to the contrary---early-token distillation creates a \emph{cascade effect} that reshapes the entire generation trajectory.

\paragraph{Late tokens change as much as early tokens.} We compare distilled and base model responses using Jaccard word similarity, split at word position 40 ($\approx$50 tokens). For pos-50, the Jaccard similarity to baseline is 0.251 for both early and late portions---late tokens, which receive zero distillation signal, change just as much as early tokens.

\paragraph{KL reduction beyond trained positions.} Pos-200 (trained on positions 0--200) reduces KL by 50--60\% within the trained range and by 30--40\% in the untrained range (positions 200--400). Table~\ref{tab:post_kl} shows these results.

\paragraph{Mechanism.} The cascade effect follows from autoregressive generation: early tokens condition the distribution over all subsequent tokens. Better early decisions---approach selection, problem framing, reasoning structure---propagate through the entire response. Distilling the reasoning strategy is sufficient because the student can already execute computation correctly; it just needs to learn \emph{which} computation to perform.

% ============================================================================
% 7. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
% ============================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why does positional distillation work?} We propose three complementary explanations. (1) \emph{Autoregressive cascade}: early tokens determine the trajectory of all subsequent tokens; better reasoning strategy propagates through the entire response. (2) \emph{Strategy over arithmetic}: the teacher's value lies in knowing \emph{how to approach} problems, not \emph{how to compute} answers; early tokens encode approach, later tokens encode computation. (3) \emph{Avoiding degenerate attractors}: after producing high-confidence answer tokens (e.g., \bboxed{}), full-sequence loss may encourage repeating them, creating a positive feedback loop that positional loss avoids by not computing loss on these positions.

\paragraph{Limitations.} Our experiments use the Qwen model family; extending to other families (Llama, Mistral) and larger scales (7B+ students) would further validate generality. We evaluate only reverse KL divergence; forward KL or other $f$-divergences may show different positional patterns. The optimal $N$ varies by task (50--200 works broadly); adaptive per-example $N$ selection could be more robust. We do not include multi-seed variance analysis.

\paragraph{Conclusion.} We showed that on-policy distillation suffers from a fundamental inefficiency: the learning signal is concentrated in the first $\sim$100 tokens, while the long tail of low-signal positions contributes noise that degrades training. \methodfull{} exploits this by restricting KD loss to the first $N$ tokens and truncating rollouts accordingly---a minimal modification that matches or exceeds full-sequence performance across math, coding, and function calling tasks while eliminating training instability. The cascade effect demonstrates that in autoregressive models, teaching the student how to begin is sufficient to improve the entire response.
